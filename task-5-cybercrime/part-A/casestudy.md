                            CASE STUDY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TITLE: "The ₹50,000 Trap — A College Student's Encounter with UPI Fraud"

WHAT IS THE CRIME?
UPI (Unified Payments Interface) fraud is a form of online financial crime in
which fraudsters manipulate victims into authorising fraudulent transactions
through deception, social engineering, or fake payment requests. Unlike
traditional theft, the victim unknowingly transfers money themselves.

HOW IT HAPPENED — STEP BY STEP:

  Step 1 — The Setup:
  Arjun, an 18-year-old first-year engineering student in Bhopal, posted his
  old laptop for sale on an online classifieds platform for ₹25,000. Within
  hours, he received a message from a person claiming to be an Army officer
  stationed in Pune, expressing urgent interest in buying the laptop.

  Step 2 — Building Trust:
  The fraudster sent a scanned copy of a fake military ID to appear credible
  and agreed to pay the full amount immediately via UPI without negotiating
  the price — a common psychological tactic to disarm suspicion.

  Step 3 — The Trick:
  Instead of sending money, the fraudster sent a UPI COLLECT REQUEST for
  ₹1 (claiming it was a "verification payment") and verbally instructed
  Arjun to "approve the request to receive money." Arjun, unfamiliar with
  the difference between sending and receiving on UPI, approved the request.

  Step 4 — Escalation:
  The fraudster then sent a second collect request for ₹25,000, again saying
  "approve this to receive your payment." Confused and pressured, Arjun
  approved it — unknowingly sending ₹25,000 from his own account.

  Step 5 — Disappearance:
  The fraudster immediately blocked Arjun on all platforms and became
  unreachable. The money was transferred to a mule account and withdrawn
  within minutes.

WHO IS USUALLY TARGETED?
  — First-time online sellers (students, young adults)
  — People unfamiliar with UPI collect request mechanics
  — Individuals in urgent financial need
  — Rural and semi-urban users with limited digital literacy
  — College students selling second-hand items on OLX, Quikr, or Facebook
    Marketplace

CONSEQUENCES:
  Financial  : Arjun lost ₹25,000 — his entire semester savings — with no
               guarantee of recovery as the funds were quickly laundered.

  Emotional  : He experienced severe anxiety, shame, and loss of confidence
               in digital transactions, affecting his academic focus.

  Legal      : Arjun filed a complaint on cybercrime.gov.in and called
               Helpline 1930. While the case was registered, fund recovery
               remained uncertain due to the use of mule accounts.

  Systemic   : Such incidents contribute to growing distrust in digital
               payment systems among young users, undermining India's
               Digital India initiative.
